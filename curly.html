The Death of the Curly Quote

In today's web pages, sightings of the curly quote (“”) have become increasingly rarer. In the early days of the World Wide Web (WWW), 7-bit ASCII did not include symbols outside of the English alphabet; including curly quotes. This made it impossible for people in countries outside the U.S. to use characters native to their language. This limitation was eventually bypassed, but required effort from the programmer and the user, which was routinely not practiced. In the early 1800's, Linotype keyboards included curly quotes. Simultaneously, practical typewriters were manufactured that did not include the curly quote. As equipment upgraded from typewriter to keyboard, so did the omission of the curly quote.

Today, the inclusion of curly quotes are dependant on algorithms and ultimately programmer decision. In the eyes of the creator, there is no reason to curl the quotation mark. Applications today (such as Apple's News feed) are forced to keep up with the formatting of the quotation mark. As Marcin Wichary, a Polish design lead at Medium says, “the lack of Polish–style quotation marks have led the current generation to use American–style quotes and think the native ones look wrong,”. The real question here is if the curly quote will become dominant once more, or if in the future people won't even realize the difference anymore.

As a visual addition, here is the same quote with different quotation marks, each taking two different levels of effort.

"That's no moon. Its a space station."
Now with curly quotes:
“That's no moon. It's a space station.”
